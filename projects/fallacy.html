Logical Fallacies Guide

Understanding common errors in reasoning and argumentation

Fallacies of Relevance

In these fallacies, the premises are logically irrelevant to the conclusion but are presented in a way that makes them appear relevant.

Appeal to the Populace

Ad Populum (R1)
When correct reasoning is replaced by devices calculated to elicit emotional and nonrational support for the conclusion urged.
Examples
"Over 10 million Americans can't be wrong! Buy our product because it's the most popular in the country."
"Everyone at school is wearing these shoes. If you want to fit in, you should get them too."

Appeal to Emotion

R2
When correct reasoning is replaced by appeals to specific emotions, such as pity, pride, or envy.
Examples
"Look at these sad photos of polar bears. You must support our environmental policy or you don't care about these innocent animals."
"As a proud American, you should buy American-made cars, regardless of their quality or price compared to foreign alternatives."

Red Herring

R3
When correct reasoning is manipulated by the introduction of some event or character that deliberately misleads the audience and thus hinders rational inference.
Examples
"Yes, our company had a data breach, but have you seen how much worse our competitor's customer service is?"
"The senator is accused of misusing campaign funds, but let's remember his years of service to the community and how he helped build that children's hospital."

Straw Man

R4
When correct reasoning is undermined by the deliberate misrepresentation of the opponent's position.
Examples
"People who support stricter gun regulations want to abolish the Second Amendment and take away all firearms from citizens."
"You want to increase the minimum wage? So you want to destroy small businesses and create massive unemployment?"

Attack on the Person

Ad Hominem (R5)
When correct reasoning about some issue is replaced by an attack upon the character or special circumstances of the opponent.
Examples
"We shouldn't listen to Dr. Smith's research on climate change because he once worked for an oil company."
"The mayor's budget proposal can't be taken seriously. Did you know she filed for bankruptcy ten years ago?"

Appeal to Force

Ad Baculum (R6)
When reasoning is replaced by threats in the effort to win support or assent.
Examples
"You should agree with my proposal if you want to keep your job at this company."
"Sign this contract now, or I'll make sure no one in this industry ever works with you again."

Missing the Point

Ignoratio Elenchi (R7)
When correct reasoning is replaced by the mistaken refutation of a position that was not really at issue.
Examples
"We shouldn't invest in renewable energy because fossil fuels have created millions of jobs throughout history."
"Why focus on reducing plastic waste when deforestation is destroying our planet's lungs?"

Fallacies of Defective Induction

In these fallacies, the premises may be relevant to the conclusion, but they are far too weak to support it.

Appeal to Ignorance

Ad Ignorantiam (D1)
When it is argued that a proposition is true on the ground that it has not been proved false, or when it is argued that a proposition is false because it has not been proved true.
Examples
"Scientists haven't proven that UFOs don't exist, so they must be real."
"There's no evidence that this supplement causes any harm, so it must be safe to consume."

Appeal to Inappropriate Authority

Ad Verecundiam (D2)
When the premises of an argument appeal to the judgment of some person or persons who have no legitimate claim to authority in the matter at hand.
Examples
"This celebrity chef says this diet pill will help you lose weight, so it must work."
"My favorite basketball player endorses this economic policy, so I think it's the right approach for our country."

False Cause

Non Causa Pro Causa (D3)
When one treats as the cause of a thing that which is not really the cause of that thing, often relying (as in the subtype post hoc ergo propter hoc) merely on the close temporal succession of two events.
Examples
"I wore my lucky socks and our team won the championship. My socks clearly brought good luck."
"Crime rates went down after we installed streetlights in the neighborhood, so the streetlights must have reduced crime."

Hasty Generalization

Converse Accident (D4)
When one moves carelessly or too quickly from one or a very few instances to a broad or universal claim.
Examples
"I know someone who went to that university and they couldn't find a job after graduating. That university must have poor job placement rates."
"The first two customers who tried our new product loved it. This proves our product will be a massive market success."

Fallacies of Presumption

In these fallacies, the mistake in argument arises from relying on some proposition that is assumed to be true but is without warrant and is false or dubious.

Accident

P1
When one mistakenly applies a generalization to an individual case that it does not properly govern.
Examples
"Freedom of speech is a right, therefore it should be legal to shout 'fire' in a crowded theater when there is no fire."
"Cutting things is generally not a crime, so surgeons shouldn't be penalized for performing surgeries."

Complex Question

Plurium Interrogationum (P2)
When one argues by asking a question in such a way as to presuppose the truth of some assumption buried in that question.
Examples
"Have you stopped cheating on your taxes?" (This presupposes that you were cheating on your taxes.)"
"Why do you continue to support policies that harm the environment?" (This presupposes that you support such policies.)"

Begging the Question

Petitio Principii (P3)
When one assumes in the premises of an argument the truth of what one seeks to establish in the conclusion of that same argument.
Examples
"This book is true because it says so in the book itself."
"God exists because the Bible says so, and the Bible is the word of God."

Fallacies of Ambiguity

In these fallacies, the mistakes in argument arise as a result of the shift in the meaning of words or phrases, from the meanings that they have in the premises to different meanings that they have in the conclusion.

Equivocation

A1
When the same word or phrase is used with two or more meanings, deliberately or accidentally, in formulating an argument.
Examples
"A bird in the hand is worth two in the bush. A bird is an animal. Therefore, any animal in the hand is worth two in the bush."
"Stars are just huge balls of gas. Famous actors are stars. Therefore, famous actors are just huge balls of gas."

Amphiboly

A2
When one of the statements in an argument has more than one plausible meaning, because of the loose or awkward way in which the words in that statement have been combined.
Examples
"The professor said on Monday we would have an exam. Therefore, the exam will be held on Monday."
"Wanted: Car for sale by owner with good tires." (Ambiguous whether the owner or the car has good tires.)

Accent

A3
When a shift of meaning arises within an argument as a consequence of changes in the emphasis given to its words or parts.
Examples
"I never said he stole the money." (Depending on which word is emphasized, this can have several different meanings.)
"We should not speak ill of our friends." (If emphasis is on "friends," it could imply that we can speak ill of others.)

Composition

A4
This fallacy is committed (a) when one reasons mistakenly from the attributes of a part to the attributes of the whole, or (b) when one reasons mistakenly from the attributes of an individual member of some collection to the attributes of the totality of that collection.
Examples
"Each player on the team is an excellent athlete. Therefore, this must be an excellent team."
"Every component of this machine is lightweight. Therefore, the entire machine must be lightweight."

Division

A5
This fallacy is committed (a) when one reasons mistakenly from the attributes of a whole to the attributes of one of its parts, or (b) when one reasons mistakenly from the attributes of a totality of some collection of entities to the attributes of the individual entities within that collection.
Examples
"The United States is the richest country in the world. Therefore, every American must be rich."
"This restaurant is excellent. Therefore, every dish they serve must be excellent."
