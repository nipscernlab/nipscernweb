What are Immediate Inferences?

Immediate inferences are logical processes where we derive a new proposition from a single given proposition without using additional premises. These transformations follow strict logical rules and preserve truth in specific ways.

A Universal Affirmative

"All S is P"

Example: All cats are mammals.

E Universal Negative

"No S is P"

Example: No reptiles are mammals.

I Particular Affirmative

"Some S is P"

Example: Some students are athletes.

O Particular Negative

"Some S is not P"

Example: Some birds are not flyers.

Conversion

Conversion interchanges the subject and predicate terms of a categorical proposition. The quality (affirmative/negative) remains the same, but quantity may change.

Original Converse Note
A: All S is P I: Some P is S By limitation
E: No S is P E: No P is S Valid
I: Some S is P I: Some P is S Valid
O: Some S is not P - Conversion not valid

Example

"All roses are flowers" converts to "Some flowers are roses"

Obversion

Obversion changes the quality of the proposition (affirmative to negative or vice versa) and replaces the predicate with its complement.

Original (Obvertend) Obverse
A: All S is P E: No S is non-P
E: No S is P A: All S is non-P
I: Some S is P O: Some S is not non-P
O: Some S is not P I: Some S is non-P

Example

"No humans are immortal" obverse is "All humans are mortal (non-immortal)"

Contraposition

Contraposition replaces both subject and predicate with their complements and interchanges them.

Original Contrapositive Note
A: All S is P A: All non-P is non-S Valid
E: No S is P O: Some non-P is not non-S By limitation
I: Some S is P - Contraposition not valid
O: Some S is not P O: Some non-P is not non-S Valid

Example

"All athletes are fit people" contrapositive is "All non-fit people are non-athletes"

Inversion

Inversion replaces both the subject and predicate terms with their complements while maintaining the original positions.

Original Inverse Note
A: All S is P O: Some non-S is not non-P By limitation
E: No S is P I: Some non-S is non-P By limitation
I: Some S is P - Inversion not valid
O: Some S is not P - Inversion not valid

Example

"All dogs are mammals" inverse is "Some non-dogs are not non-mammals"

Equipollence

Equipollence is the process of determining logical equivalence between propositions. Two propositions are equipollent if they have the same truth value.

Proposition Equipollent With
A: All S is P E: No S is non-P (its obverse)
E: No S is P A: All S is non-P (its obverse)
I: Some S is P O: Some S is not non-P (its obverse)
O: Some S is not P I: Some S is non-P (its obverse)
A: All S is P A: All non-P is non-S (its contrapositive)

Example

"All triangles are polygons" is equipollent with "No triangles are non-polygons"

Key Relationships

  • Contraries (A & E): Cannot both be true, but both can be false
  • Subcontraries (I & O): Cannot both be false, but both can be true
  • Contradictories (A & O, E & I): One must be true, one must be false
  • Subalternation (A → I, E → O): If universal is true, particular must be true

Immediate Inferences Overview

  • Conversion: Interchange subject and predicate
  • Obversion: Change quality, replace predicate with complement
  • Contraposition: Negate and interchange subject and predicate
  • Inversion: Replace both terms with complements
  • Equipollence: Establishing logical equivalence
